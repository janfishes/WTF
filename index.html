Season Guide

Each species row shows the 12 months of the year, January through December. Brighter cells mean a better bite that month; dim cells are the slow season. These are general patterns for the Ponce de Leon Inlet region — not a substitute for a current tide chart or fishing report. Many species have closed seasons and size, bag, and gear limits. Refer to the Fish Rules App (fishrulesapp.com) for current state and federal regulations and help identifying fish.

Offshore sites

Reef site coordinates are centroids computed from FWC's Artificial Reef Program deployment records for Volusia County (191 individual drops grouped into 16 sites, data as of Mar 6, 2026). Tap a reef pin and hit "Show drops" to see every individual deployment with its coordinates, listed closest-to-farthest from the inlet. Sites 4, 9, 10 & 11 sit 17–34nm out and are outside the default view; pan/zoom to find them.

Every catch you log shows up here and as a diamond marker on the map, colored by species. Use ✎ to edit an entry or ✕ to delete it. Export backup saves your whole log to a file named wtf-log-backup.json, and Import backup reads that same file back in — on this device, another one, or a fresh copy of the app.

Map controls

My Catches (first in the row) shows/hides your catch diamonds. Inshore and Offshore are on/off switches for their zone's fishing pins and labels — turn one off to hide that zone entirely. Launches shows/hides the boat ramp and kayak launch pins, independently of everything else. Labels turns the pill names on or off for whatever pins are showing — off means no labels, but pins stay. The species buttons down the left side filter your catch log: tap one and the map shows only your catch diamonds for that species — the fishing-spot pins step aside until you switch it back off. A picked species stays visible at any zoom, and shows even if My Catches is off. If you've never logged that fish, you'll get a note saying so. Locate Me drops your GPS position (tap again to remove it). All coordinates display in degrees, minutes, seconds.

Moving a popup or panel out of the way

Any popup that opens on the map — a spot, a catch, a reef with its drops showing — can be dragged anywhere on screen so it isn't covering the pins underneath. On a phone: press and hold anywhere on the popup that isn't a button, then slide your finger; the popup follows and stays put when you lift off. On a computer: click and hold anywhere on the popup that isn't a button or link, then drag. Buttons, links and the scrolling drops list still work normally, so you can drag from the popup's title or body text. The 3-Day Tide Forecast panel moves the same way — drag it by its title bar.

Log a catch

Tap + Catch and pick the species from the list. Set date and time (shown in local 12-hour time), pick the tide by hand (High, Low, Falling, or Rising) or tap Auto-detect from NOAA once a location and date/time are set — the app looks up the nearest NOAA tide station's predictions for that exact moment (works for past dates too; needs internet), and set the location three ways: choose a known spot from the alphabetical list, Drop pin on map, or Use my location. Clear removes a dropped pin. Length, weight, and notes are optional.

Photos

Tap the photo box to attach a photo from your gallery — photos are stored on your device at high quality. If the photo has embedded GPS and a timestamp, the app fills in the catch location, date, and time automatically, and looks up the tide for that moment from NOAA. If it doesn't (photos sent through messaging apps usually lose this data), just set the date and location yourself, then tap the tide buttons or NOAA auto-detect — everything stays editable before you save. Photos need to be in a format your browser can open — JPEG always works. Safari can read iPhone HEIC files, but Chrome, Edge and Firefox can't; if a photo is refused, set Settings → Camera → Formats → Most Compatible on your iPhone so new photos save as JPEG.

Edit or delete a catch

In the + Catch sheet, the Location list holds every known fishing spot — inshore and offshore, plus any spot you've added yourself. Boat ramps and kayak launches are left out, since they're put-in points rather than places you fish; use Drop pin or My location if you did catch something at one. The Inshore and Offshore switches just above the list narrow it down when you're only fishing one of them.

Tap a catch diamond on the map and use ✎ Edit or Delete in its popup — or use the ✎ and ✕ buttons on any entry in the My Log sheet. Editing reopens the form with everything filled in.

Add your own detail to a pin

Tap any pin, then tap ＋ Add my note at the bottom of its popup. Type your details — bait, rig, tide, timing — and Save. Notes persist between sessions and can be edited or cleared anytime with ✎ Edit my note.

Reef drops

Offshore pins have a Show drops button that lists every individual deployment for the county reefs (CR) and for clustered natural reef sites — coordinates in degrees/minutes/seconds, depth, and distance — sorted closest to farthest from the inlet, and plots them on the map while the popup is open. Each drop pin is numbered to match its position in the list, so you can verify GPS numbers pin-by-pin. If a popup covers the pins, drag it out of the way by its title area.

Themes

Tap 🎨 to pick a color theme (dark options, light, and beige) — saved on your device. As you zoom out, pins collapse into count pills that sit just below the top buttons — one under each matching button — showing totals for spots, sites, launches, and catches. Tap a pill or zoom back in to expand them.

Search

Tap the 🔍 button next to Help (top right) and type a location name (e.g. "snook alley"), a launch (e.g. "swoop"), or a species (e.g. "pompano"). Results show matching location pins, every spot tagged for that species, and your own logged catches of it. Tap any result to fly to it on the map with its popup open — hidden pins are switched back on automatically.

Reading the month bars

Every colored bar row you see — in a pin's popup or the Season Guide — is a calendar: 12 cells, January on the left through December on the right. Brighter cells = better months for that species at that spot. In popups, the letters above the bars mark the months.

Tides (works offline)

Tap Street Map (map, bottom-right) to switch between the satellite and street-map view. Tap Tides, then tap anywhere to drop a Tide pin — you get a 3-day high/low forecast, built from official NOAA predictions for Ponce Inlet stored on your device (through July 2027). Instead of just borrowing the nearest station's timing, the app measures how far your pin sits along the same waterway and interpolates the tide lag by distance between the calibrated stations on that arm — or extrapolates past the last one when you're farther upstream. Each forecast carries a confidence badge: High when you're on or between calibrated stations, and Low (with a ⚠) when the timing is extrapolated, off the mapped channel, or in a shallow wind-driven reach. No internet needed. Stations north of the inlet (Spruce Creek, Rose Bay, Dunlawton) are calibrated; the wind-driven lagoon reaches to the south have no firm calibration yet, so those spots show low-confidence Ponce Inlet timing.

Using it offline

The app itself — all your spots, launches, reefs, catch log, notes, tide forecasts, search, and themes — works with no internet. The map imagery normally streams from map servers, but you can now download it for your fishing area so even the satellite view works with the signal off — do it once on wifi and the tiles are stored on your device. Add the app to your home screen for the most reliable offline launch.

Tips

On a phone, use Add to Home Screen to launch it like an app. Each person's catches and notes stay private on their own device. Use Export backup in the My Log sheet to save your log as a file, and Import backup to bring it into a new copy or another device.

Calibrate tide lags

Turn a station's recorded water level into a real timing offset. The app already stores the inlet's NOAA high/low times, so this works offline once you paste the data. Pick a station, paste (or upload) a Stevens-Connect export, and tap Compute. If it looks solid, Save it — the tide panel will use it right away.

—

Needs a timestamp and a water-level column (extra columns like sigma/temp are fine — it finds the level column). Best export: Normalized interval or Raw readings in CSV at 6–15 min. Avoid hourly/daily aggregates (too coarse to time a high/low). NOAA CO-OPS / Ready formats work too, but are usually UTC — tick the box below. On the dashboard: station → Exports (or Trends) → 1w or a custom 1–2 week range. Two weeks gives the most trustworthy result.

Offline map

Download the satellite map — USGS Imagery Topo over the land and Sentinel-2 imagery over the water — for the whole fishing area (Ponce Inlet, the Halifax and Indian Rivers, Mosquito Lagoon, and the offshore reefs). Do this once while you have wifi; afterward the map draws with the signal off. Tiles are stored on this device only.

Zoom 16 (closest) isn't offered for download — at well over a gigabyte it's too large to store reliably on a phone. The saved levels cover navigation and spot-finding; if you're offline and zoom in closer than the saved level, the imagery may go blank until you zoom back out or reconnect.
